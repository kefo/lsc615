Assignment 2 - Metadata Schema Development (30% of overall grade)


As a group, you will design a metadata schema for a particular type of content. It will be important to identify, very early, the "who" and "what" questions this project raises. Who will be using your metadata schema, why, and what information must you capture in order to serve those users and their needs? Recognize that not all of your metadata's users will be patrons looking for content. Users will also likely be catalogers, reference librarians, and IT staffs, all of whom may have different requirements of the data (if not the actual object the data describes). What do all of these users need in order to manage the resource? Are there rules and guidelines implementers should follow in order to best achieve specific goals, which may range from a user searching a system to two libraries exchanging records. In short, remember that not all metadata is descriptive.


You need to create a "primer" document that introduces and provides a high-level overview of your schema. In it, you should lay out answers - at a high-level - to all the facets mentioned above and any other you think of as you progress. You will also need to fashion an XML format for your metadata. As with all good metadata schemas, you will need to define each of the elements and attributes used in your schema. Think of it as an element/attribute dictionary. Your elements/attributes and the overall construction of your XML should make semantic sense. For example, if you feel the need to create an element for an "alternate" or "variant" title, it would be unwise to call that element "secondTitle" because you could have made a better semantic choice, one that would have been more intuitive to an implementer. (For the record, I know in class I said you may only need 15 elements, but we also know how far 15 elements gets us, which is to say, not very far at all. I expect you will need to define a good many more than 15 elements/attributes to describe your chosen content types.) By now you also know how important example records are, so you will need to include example records. Although you do not need to create a formal XML schema (XSD), which could be used for validation purposes, your example XML records should be consistent from one to another (for example, if you use birthDate - uppercase 'D' - in one example record, it would be wrong to use birthdate - lowercase 'd' - in another example record). Of course, all of your XML examples should be well-formed. Ultimately, someone who encounters your introductory document, element/attribute dictionary, and example records should be able to gain a good understanding of what your metadata schema is designed for and for whom. Each group will give a short presentation (10 minutes) about its metadata schema during our last on-campus class. 90% of this assignment's grade will be based on these group outputs.


The final 10 percent of your grade will be a short 400-500 word essay in which you should address any challenges to the project. This is an individual aspect of this assignment, not a group component, so each student will complete his/her own essay. It will be turned in separately from the group parts, by way of upload through Blackboard "assignment" section. I'm absolutely not interested in a "so-and-so did this and so-and-so did that and the project would have been better...." I'm interested in you addressing the human component that comes with the creation of metadata schemas, specifically with an eye toward the respective roles humans and technology play when it comes to metadata schema creation, and success.


As for the form and format of your schema, I ask: What worked best for you? (And I do not just mean digital format, such as HTML or PDF, but also content form, such a the difference between how VRA presented its schema and how MODS presented its schema). If it worked for you, surely it would work for others. You can provide me with PDFs or Word Documents (we've used documentation in those forms this semester) or you can post them online as HTML, along with the examples (do not give me full XML examples in a Word document - we're not going there! - snippets, such as we saw with VRA, are fine, of course). I, personally, have a preference for HTML documentation because I would rather open a web page versus a PDF document, but I recognize there are a myriad of ways to go about this. Either way, I would like you to make your material available to everyone - so you'll upload it to a forum or provide us all with links to your mini-website (documentation, examples). However it comes to me, I should be able to read the primer, read the element/attribute dictionary, and view your examples and then I should be able to create my own XML record from it.
